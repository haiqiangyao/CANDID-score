    Circadian Syndrome (CircS) Overview

    What is CircS?

    Circadian Syndrome (CircS) is a clinical framework that systematically captures the metabolic consequences of circadian rhythm disruption, first proposed by Zimmet et al. in 2019. It extends the traditional Metabolic Syndrome (MetS) by adding two behaviorally-grounded circadian components — sleep deprivation and depressive symptoms — alongside five core metabolic criteria. CircS thereby bridges chronobiology with clinical metabolic medicine, recognizing that disrupted biological rhythms are not merely a lifestyle inconvenience but a systemic driver of cardiometabolic disease.

    CircS Diagnostic Criteria

    CircS is diagnosed when ≥ 4 of the following 7 components are present simultaneously:

    ComponentCriterion
    HG – HyperglycemiaFBG ≥ 100 mg/dL or antidiabetic medication
    HTN – HypertensionSBP ≥ 130 mmHg or DBP ≥ 85 mmHg, or antihypertensive medication
    HTG – HypertriglyceridemiaTG ≥ 150 mg/dL or lipid-lowering medication
    CO – Central ObesityWC ≥ 102 cm (male) / ≥ 88 cm (female) [Caucasian]; ≥ 90 cm (male) / ≥ 80 cm (female) [Asian]
    LHDL – Low HDL-CHDL-C < 40 mg/dL (male) or < 50 mg/dL (female)
    SD – Sleep DeprivationAverage nightly sleep ≤ 7 hours
    DEP – Depressive SymptomsPHQ-9 > 4 (Caucasian) or CES-D-10 > 10 (Asian)

    In NHANES (n=17,666), 37.8% of participants met CircS criteria. CircS-positive individuals had dramatically higher rates of T2D (33.4% vs 7.9%), hypertension (92.5% vs 56.4%), and obesity (59.3% vs 26.7%) compared to CircS-negative individuals. Middle-aged adults comprised 44.1% of the CircS-positive group.

    CircS as a Predictor and Causal Driver of Type 2 Diabetes

    The central question our research addressed: does CircS causally increase T2D risk, or merely co-occur with it? Using four complementary analytical strategies across two independent national cohorts — NHANES (n=17,666, USA) and CHARLS (n=10,795, China) — we established both the predictive power and causal role of CircS in T2D development.

    ① Cross-Sectional Evidence — Multivariable Logistic Regression

    After fully adjusting for age, sex, education, income, marital status, race/ethnicity, and smoking, CircS-positive individuals showed substantially elevated odds of prevalent T2D in both cohorts. These associations were statistically robust and replicated across two ethnically and geographically distinct populations.

    ② Longitudinal Evidence — Cox Proportional Hazards Regression

    In NHANES participants followed through 2018, multivariable Cox regression confirmed that baseline CircS independently predicted future incident T2D. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis demonstrated a significantly accelerated decline in diabetes-free survival among CircS-positive individuals throughout the follow-up period, with progressively widening separation between groups.

    Stratified subgroup analyses revealed a striking age-dependent pattern: the relative T2D risk amplification by CircS was significantly more pronounced in young and middle-aged adults than in elderly populations (interaction P < 0.05). This disproportionate vulnerability in younger demographics likely reflects intensified modern circadian disruptors — including pervasive artificial light at night and irregular meal timing — acting during a critical window of metabolic plasticity.

    ③ Mechanistic Evidence — Mediation Analysis

    Mediation analyses in NHANES identified insulin resistance and hepatic steatosis as the primary biological pathways through which CircS drives T2D. These two interconnected mechanisms collectively mediate more than half of the total CircS-to-T2D effect, revealing specific therapeutic targets for intervention.

    ④ Causal Evidence — Two-Sample Mendelian Randomization

    To move beyond observational association, we performed two-sample Mendelian randomization (MR) using large-scale GWAS data from the FinnGen consortium (n>460,000). Genetically predicted dietary rhythm regularity — a validated genetic proxy for circadian behavioral integrity — served as the exposure instrument. Independent GWAS summary statistics for obesity and T2D from FinnGen served as outcomes. A total of 21 independent SNPs were used for the obesity analysis and 20 for T2D.

    Causal estimates were highly consistent across four MR methods — inverse-variance weighted (IVW), weighted median, simple mode, and weighted mode — confirming robustness to outliers and pleiotropy. This establishes a unidirectional causal axis: disruption of circadian dietary rhythms → obesity → type 2 diabetes, providing genetic-level evidence that circadian disruption is a genuine upstream cause of T2D, not merely a metabolic correlate.

    From CircS to CANDID: Why a Weighted Index?

    Although CircS reliably identifies high-risk individuals, its original binary definition (≥ 4 of 7 components) implicitly treats all seven components as equally diabetogenic. This assumption is biologically untenable: hyperglycemia — the defining feature of diabetes itself — should carry far greater predictive weight than sleep duration or mood symptoms. Explainable machine learning (XGBoost + SHAP) allows us to quantify each component's individualized contribution, resolving this equivalence assumption.

    SHAP-Derived Component Weights (XGBoost trained on NHANES, n=14,071; validated on CHARLS, n=10,795)

    HG 0.428 → 20 pts HTN 0.190 → 9 pts HTG 0.151 → 7 pts CO 0.137 → 6 pts LHDL 0.049 → 2 pts SD 0.023 → 1 pt DEP 0.022 → 1 pt

    Hyperglycemia (HG) accounts for 42.8% of the total predictive weight — nearly double the second-ranked component (HTN, 19.0%). Sleep deprivation and depressive symptoms, absent from traditional MetS, together contribute incremental yet statistically significant risk information that improves discrimination beyond metabolic markers alone.

    Model Details

    CANDID Score Calculation

    The CANDID Score is derived from seven CircS components. Each component is weighted according to its SHAP feature importance derived from XGBoost models trained across both cohorts. Components are individually scored as binary (0 or 1) indicators and then weighted.
